Two of the outliers that both defy easy classification, yet clearly constitute a group, are John Robb's Global Guerillas and Dmitri Orlov's ClubOrlov.

John Robb is the author of Brave New War: The Next Stage of Terrorism and the End of Globalization, and the chief proselytizer of the Resilient Communities meme, which basically says that much of the world will soon be like much of Iraq - as he puts it, a "Bazaar of Violence" - and the only way you're going to have any say in your lifestyle (and, you know, life) in the future is either to engineer a an artificial tribe (a corporation and/or gang) or to design and live in a small town that is aware of its role as a source of its resident's livelihood.  Generate your own power, grow your own food, issue your own currency, all that.

So, like Ron Paul people, but not wasting time running for office or complaining about the federal reserve, etc.  He lays it out pretty simply: the interconnected markets of the world are a more reactive and complex system than any single government, or any coalition of governments that could form a practical coalition, and therefore, no government or coalition can control or re-direct it.  So hang on tight for a swift descent into a new phase of...something! 

Dmitri Orlov wrote a book called Reinventing Collapse: The Soviet Example and American Prospects
, which is basically about what advice he, as a victim of the hollowing out and looting of the Soviet Government, can give to us, future survivors of the American Collapse.  Yeah, he says it's already happening...well, yes, but he says people living in Russia at the time didn't see it coming either...well of course they tried to...now look, I don't see what my mother...FINE SURE I'M A COMMUNIST GET OVER IT.

I should also say that this book came out in June of last year and has recently, shall we say, seen it's stock rise significantly.

So, what these two sites have in common is
  1. They are both shall we say mildly pessimistic about the chances many current economic and societal structures have for survival in the coming years, much less decades. 
  2. I frequently find them both to be hilarious.  
Don't get me wrong: I believe them.  I've been convinced.  It may not happen as fast or as bad as they say, but I rate many of their predictions as above 60% likely.  But for all the bleakness and sadness I acknowledge I should be feeling, the idea, for example, that I might see in my lifetime the collapse of Northern Mexico and parts of the Southern US into narco-feudal warzones makes me completely giddy.  I start giggling.  Explanations of the expected hollowing out of nation-states make me guffaw.  I couldn't help it, and for a while, I didn't even question it.

Discussions with others (frequently started by me sharing a post in reader about, say, "international corporations successfully capturing vast state assets" with a note of, say, "HAW HAW") has gradually revealed to me that this is not a universal reaction.  They find the concept of Black Swans - events that are about to happen for the first time and therefore cannot be predicted based on previously gathered data - quite frightening.  And they should!  It's going to be terrible!  Swathes of people are going to die (elsewhere) or at best learn how to live on drastically reduced resources (here)!  How can I laugh at that?

But I do, so I was encouraged by people who I tried to share my mirth with to explain myself.

I think my problem is that, having accepted predictions of unavoidable awfulness, I feel compelled to not let it effect me unproductively.  The surprise of seeing things this way induces shock, and laughter is the noise we instinctively make when we are shocked but rapidly become convinced that we aren't in immediate danger, or that at the very least (it seems in my case) that we should try not to lose our heads just yet.

I'd be afraid of the future if it were looming behind me as I type this, but as it is instead looming over the entire world, I accept that I have to roll with the punches. It's not that the situation is humorous; humor exploits the laughter response, rather than creating it.  The situation is a communal threat that I respond to with a socially-focused quasi-distress call.  I'm trying to be aware of what a global depression would require of me, and of all the steps I can to be ready for a collapse.

But the very first thing that happens when I realize that the United States doesn't need to have a command economy to reliably produce "solutions" that just make things worse, is I start to laugh.  It's not at anyone's expense; it's just in surprise at the sudden appearance of a serious challenge that's facing all of us.

Part of the cause of this perception is that people of my age or younger, even in DC, seem to have a bias against talking too seriously with each other.  Yes, the college I attended trained its young people to talk endlessly about everything, but even in my fellow students, I saw a push towards not being a downer, towards shared frivolity.

And that's not bad.  It's just that now, I feel more inclined to share my laughter with people than what comes in the silence afterwards, the fumbling strategic planning of a young person who doesn't have any sources of information on the subject except these two doom-sayers.

Well, them and Thomas P. Barnett, author of (among others) Great Powers: America and the World After Bush, now available in better bookstores nationwide.  His is the hopeful approach.

I ended up putting all three of them in a folder informatively labeled "Future," and none in the folder labeled "Humor."

-Nick
