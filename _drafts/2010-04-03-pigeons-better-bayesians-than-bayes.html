--- layout: post title: Pigeons Better Bayesians than Bayes date: '2010-04-03T11:30:00.001-04:00' author: Nick Novitski tags: modified_time: '2010-04-03T15:25:45.044-04:00' blogger_id: tag:blogger.com,1999:blog-31707300.post-6476115608497894513 --- I've been wanting to write a post about the Monty Hall problem for a long while, and luckily, some stupid birds have given me a great excuse.

Perhaps you've never heard of the Monty Hall problem?  So long ago, on the game show Let's Make A Deal, hosted by a man named Monty Hall, there was a recurring game where the contestants were given three doors to choose between.  Behind one was nothing.  Behind one was a new car.  Behind one was an old goat.  So Monty would invite the contestant to pick a door.  But then it gets unexpected.  After they had chosen a door, Monty would then open one of the doors they had not picked, showing them what was behind it.  Now there were two closed doors, one of which the contestant had indicated earlier.  And then Monty would ask if they wanted to stick with that door as their choice, or if they wanted to switch to the other one.

That isn't the Monty Hall problem though.  I mean, some people call it that, but I don't.  I just call that "playing a particular game on Let's Make a Deal."  In my opinion, the real Monty Hall problem is how many people get the question wrong.  I mean, of course I did, but brilliant people did, too.

But you don't have to be like them.  Prepare to learn the secret!

I'm currently thinking of a number from 1 to a thousand.  If you can guess it, I'll give you five bucks.  Go ahead and say your guess aloud.  Okay, now, I admit that it's either the number you said, or it's 218.  So of those two, which do you think it is now?
